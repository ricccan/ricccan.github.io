Measures of Location and Dispersion

Common types, formulas, uses, advantages and limitations

Introduction

In statistics, measures of location (also called measures of central tendency) summarize the "center" or typical value of a dataset. Measures of dispersion describe how spread out the data are around that center. Both families of measures are required to understand a dataset: a single central value without a measure of spread is usually insufficient to characterise the data.

Common measures of location (central tendency)

1. Arithmetic mean (sample & population)

The arithmetic mean (average) is the sum of observations divided by the number of observations. It is the most commonly used measure of center.

Mean (population): μ = (1/N) Σi=1N xi
Mean (sample): \(\bar{x} = \dfrac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n x_i\)

Uses: Good for quantitative, symmetric data; used in many statistical methods (e.g., least squares).
Advantages: Efficient estimator under many models, easy to compute and to manipulate analytically.
Limitations: Sensitive to outliers and skewed distributions.

2. Median

The median is the middle value when observations are ordered. For an even number of observations it is commonly the average of the two middle values.

Median: middle order statistic (50th percentile)

Uses: Preferred in skewed distributions or when outliers are present (e.g., incomes, house prices).
Advantages: Robust to outliers; reflects a typical value in skewed data.
Limitations: Less efficient than the mean for symmetric, light-tailed data; harder to manipulate analytically.

3. Mode

The mode is the most frequently occurring value(s) in the dataset. For continuous data, modes can be estimated from density peaks.

Mode: argmax of frequency / density

Uses: Useful for categorical data or to identify common values (e.g., most common category, common defect).
Advantages: Intuitive for categorical variables; unaffected by extreme numeric values.
Limitations: May not be unique; for continuous variables it is less well-defined and sensitive to smoothing choices.

4. Other location measures (brief)

  • Trimmed mean: Mean after removing a fixed percentage of the smallest and largest observations (robust compromise between mean and median).
  • Weighted mean: Mean where observations have weights (useful when samples have different importances).
  • Geometric mean / Harmonic mean: Useful for multiplicative processes or rates (e.g., growth rates).

Common measures of dispersion (spread)

1. Range

The range is the difference between maximum and minimum: range = max(x) - min(x).

Uses: Quick indicator of spread.
Advantages: Simple to compute and understand.
Limitations: Highly sensitive to outliers; ignores distribution shape.

2. Variance and Standard Deviation

Variance measures average squared deviation from the mean; standard deviation (SD) is its square root and is in the same units as the data.

Population variance: σ2 = (1/N)Σ(xi - μ)2
Sample variance: s2 = (1/(n-1))Σ(xi - x̄)2
Standard deviation: s = √(s2)

Uses: Foundational in inference, hypothesis testing, confidence intervals, regression and many models assume or use variance/SD.
Advantages: Mathematically convenient; relates to least-squares methods; captures overall dispersion.
Limitations: Sensitive to outliers; because it squares deviations, extreme values have large influence.

3. Interquartile Range (IQR)

IQR = Q3 − Q1, the difference between the 75th and 25th percentiles. It measures the spread of the middle 50% of data.

Uses: Robust measure of spread when data contains outliers or is skewed; used to detect outliers (e.g., Tukey's fences).
Advantages: Resistant to extreme values; easy to interpret.
Limitations: Ignores tails of the distribution; less efficient for symmetric, light-tailed distributions compared to SD.

4. Median Absolute Deviation (MAD)

MAD = median(|xi − median(x)|). A robust measure of variability often scaled by a constant (≈1.4826) to estimate SD under normality.

Uses: Robust alternative to SD for outlier-prone data.
Advantages: Very robust; simple concept.
Limitations: Less efficient if data are normal.

5. Coefficient of Variation (CV)

CV = SD / mean. It is a dimensionless measure that expresses variability relative to the mean.

Uses: Comparing variability across datasets with different units or scales (e.g., comparing variation in different sensors).
Advantages: Unitless and comparable across datasets.
Limitations: Not defined if the mean is near zero or exactly zero; sensitive to translation of data.

6. Percentiles and quantiles

Percentiles (e.g., 10th, 90th) describe locations in the distribution and together can characterize spread and tail behavior.

When to use which measure — guidance and examples

Symmetric, light-tailed data (approximately normal)

  • Location: Arithmetic mean is efficient and preferred.
  • Dispersion: Standard deviation and variance are natural choices and integrate well with parametric methods (t-tests, ANOVA, regression).

Skewed data or data with outliers

  • Location: Median or trimmed mean is preferable because they are robust to extreme values.
  • Dispersion: IQR or MAD provide robust measures of spread; do not rely only on range or SD.

Categorical data

  • Location: Mode (most frequent category) is appropriate.
  • Dispersion: Use frequency tables, entropy, or measures like diversity indices rather than variance/SD.

Comparing variability across datasets with different scales

  • Use coefficient of variation to compare relative variability when means differ, but be cautious if means are near zero.

Small samples

  • Sample estimates can be noisy; prefer robust measures if outliers likely; use corrected sample variance formula (divide by n−1) for unbiased variance estimate.

Practical recommendations

  • Always report at least one measure of location and one measure of dispersion (e.g., mean ± SD or median + IQR).
  • Look at data visually (histogram, boxplot, density) before choosing statistics — plots reveal skewness and outliers.
  • For skewed/outlier-prone data prefer median + IQR or trimmed mean + robust SD; for roughly normal data prefer mean ± SD.
  • Be explicit whether estimates are for the sample or population and which formula you used (e.g., sample variance with n−1 denominator).
  • When comparing groups, use scale-appropriate measures (CV for relative comparison, absolute SD for absolute differences).
Short reference: formulas shown are the standard definitions; choose robust vs classical measures depending on distribution shape, sample size, and purpose (description vs inference).
